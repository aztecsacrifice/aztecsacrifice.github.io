Atl Caualo

"ceasing of water"

First, a feast was celebrated for the Tlalocs (rain gods).
Blood sacrifices were made at Tepetzinco, or at Pantitlan -- here, they left consecrated papers and set up long poles called cuenmnantli. Here, they took the children known as "human banners" -- those chiddren who had two cowlicks whose day signs were favorable. They were thought to be the most precious blood-offerings, and were given to the rain gods in hope of rain. Everywhere they ereced cuenmantli, on which they placed white paper flags dotted with drops of liquid rubber.
They took the children to many different places:
First, Quahtepec. The one who died here bore the same name "Quautepetl"; his paper vestments were brown.
Second, Mount Yoaltecatl. This child was named "Yoltecatl"; his paper vestman was black striped with red.
Third, Tepetzinco. This child was named "Quetzalxock", dressed in blue.
Fourth, Poyauhtlan, at the foot of Mount Tepetzinco. This child was named "Poyauhtecatl" and adorned in stripes of liquid rubber.
Fifth, a place in the middle of a lake, Pantitlan. This child was called "Epcoactl"; his vestments were set with mussel shells.
Sixth, the top of the hill of Cocotl. This child was named "Cocotl"; he was dressed in both red and brown.
Seventh, on the summit of Yiahqueme. This child bore the name "Yiahqueme", and his clothes were completely brown.
All of these children wore head-band with "sprays and sprigs of quetzal feathers; they had green stone necklaces, and they went provided with green stone bracelets...Their faces were painted with liquid rubber, and spotted with a paste of amaranth seeds...they had sandals of liquid rubber...they gave them paper wings...they were carried in litteeers covered with quetzal feathers...and they went sounding flutes for them"
During this time, everyone mourned loudly. It was said that if the children began to cry, they rain would indeed begin.
The children were brought to Yopico, Totex's temple, where they would participate in a mock battle in the same way they would later die -- as they would tear their hearts out of them.
Four times these children appeared before the people:
First time, they were dressed in red.
Second time, they were dressed in white.
Third time, red again.
And fourth, again, white. After this, the children were taken and slain.
